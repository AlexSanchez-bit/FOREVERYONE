To get the best results from the Firefly Algorithm, you need to fine-tune its core performance parameters. By carefully adjusting the absorption coefficient and randomness, you can significantly improve convergence rates, avoid local minima, and enhance the algorithm's global search capabilities.

## Understanding the core parameters

The Firefly Algorithm relies on three main parameters to dictate how fireflies interact and move through the search space. 

| Parameter | Symbol | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Randomness** | `Îą` (alpha) | Controls the step size of a firefly's random walk. Higher values mean more erratic, exploratory movement. |
| **Base Attractiveness** | `Îē0` (beta) | The attractiveness of a firefly at a distance of zero. This is typically kept constant at `1.0`. |
| **Absorption Coefficient** | `Îģ` (gamma) | Determines how quickly light intensity decreases over distance. It controls the balance between local and global search. |

## Tuning the Absorption Coefficient (Îģ)

The absorption coefficient (`Îģ`) is critical for determining how far a firefly can "see" others. Our testing on high-dimensional benchmark functions (like Zakharov and Colville) reveals a direct trade-off when setting this value.

* **Low absorption (e.g., `Îģ = 0.1`):** A lower value means light travels further. This strongly favors **global exploration**, allowing fireflies to discover distant, potentially better regions of the search space.
* **High absorption (e.g., `Îģ = 1.0`):** Light degrades quickly, restricting fireflies to local interactions. 

> [!WARNING]
> High `Îģ` values can cause premature stagnation in non-optimal regions (local minima). If your specific use case requires a high absorption coefficient, you must increase your overall population size to compensate for the reduced exploration range.

## Managing Randomness (Îą) dynamically

While a static randomness value can work for simple problems, **dynamic reduction** of `Îą` is highly recommended for complex, high-dimensional functions like Styblinski-Tang.

Instead of keeping `Îą` constant, start with a higher value to encourage broad exploration in the early stages of the algorithm. As the optimization progresses towards the final stages, dynamically reduce `Îą`. This allows the algorithm to transition from broad exploration to precise fine-tuning, achieving remarkable accuracy when closing in on known global minima.

## Recommended tuning workflow

Follow these steps to systematically tune your algorithm for a new dataset or function:

<Steps>
<Step title="Establish a baseline">
Start with standard default values: set base attractiveness (`Îē0`) to `1.0`, randomness (`Îą`) to `1.0`, and absorption (`Îģ`) to `1.0`.
</Step>
<Step title="Optimize for global search">
Lower the absorption coefficient (`Îģ`) to a value between `0.1` and `0.5`. Run your tests and observe if the algorithm finds better global regions compared to your baseline.
</Step>
<Step title="Implement dynamic randomness">
Modify your algorithm's loop to gradually decrease `Îą` over time (for example, starting at `0.5` or `1.0` and decaying towards `0.01` by the final iteration).
</Step>
<Step title="Evaluate and adjust population">
If the algorithm is converging too early on suboptimal solutions, either lower `Îģ` further or increase the total number of fireflies in your population.
</Step>
</Steps>
